Who Complains — and Can We See It Coming?

The short answer: Static profile alone doesn't predict complaints — 82% of accounts file a ticket regardless of plan or tenure. But there is a reliable early warning: platform activity spikes 1.6× in the 30 days before a first ticket in 45% of Enterprise and Free accounts.

Key Findings
✅ Confirmed — H4

Activity spikes before complaints

In the 30 days before filing a first ticket, accounts generate 40% more events than in the prior 30-day baseline period. In 45% of measured accounts, the spike exceeds 1.5×. This is a sustained buildup, not a last-minute burst — the final 7 days look identical to baseline. Holds in Enterprise (42%) and Free (56%); marginal in Pro (38%).
💡 Secondary signal

Ticket type varies sharply by plan

Free accounts file predominantly billing tickets (8 of 23 = 35%). Pro accounts almost exclusively file feature requests (10 of 24 = 42%) with virtually no billing issues (1 ticket). Enterprise spreads evenly across all categories. This allows support routing by plan type before reading ticket content.
⚠️ Weak — H2

Enterprise complains more, but not 2× more

Enterprise accounts have a 94.7% complaint rate vs ~74% for Pro and Free. The gap is real, but at only 1.16× the overall average it falls well short of the 2× threshold required to call plan tier a reliable predictor. Nearly every Enterprise account files a ticket — suggesting this is a usage norm, not a signal.
🔍 Open question

May 2025 spike unexplained

May 2025 saw 21 tickets across 16 accounts — 2.1× the monthly average of 10. No product changelog, incident log, or billing cycle data is available in the schema to explain the cause. Notably, May accounts had weaker pre-signals than other months (excluding May actually strengthens the H4 ratio to 2.26×). Root cause investigation is recommended.
Recommendations
1

Build a 30-day rolling activity monitor for Enterprise and Free accounts

When an account's event volume in the past 30 days exceeds 1.5× its prior 30-day average, trigger a proactive CS outreach touchpoint. This signal is detectable before the ticket is filed. Start with Enterprise and Free — where the signal is confirmed. Do not rely on this for Pro accounts without further data.

2

Route incoming tickets by plan before reading content

Free accounts → billing team; Pro accounts → product team (feature requests dominate); Enterprise → general queue (even spread). This reduces triage time for 70%+ of tickets that follow the pattern.

3

Investigate the May 2025 ticket spike

21 tickets in one month (2.1× average) affected 16 distinct accounts. Cross-reference with the product changelog, infrastructure incident log, and billing renewal dates for May. Identifying the root cause will either surface a recurring risk or close out the open question.

4

Extend the pre-signal analysis to event types

The 30-day activity buildup is confirmed on volume. The next analysis should break down which types of events spike before a ticket — error events, failed actions, repeat navigation? This would sharpen the early warning model from "more activity" to "more of this specific behavior."

Caveats & Limitations
Small dataset: 50 accounts total; 9 non-complainers. Binary complainer/non-complainer comparisons are statistically fragile. All conclusions should be treated as directional until replicated on a larger dataset.
H4 window dependency: The pre-signal holds at 30 days but weakens below threshold at 14 days (ratio 1.38×, spike% 34%). The 30-day window is well-calibrated; shorter detection windows will miss the signal.
H4 plan scope: The pre-signal is confirmed for Enterprise and Free accounts. Pro accounts show a marginal result (1.33×, 38%) — below both thresholds. Do not apply the early warning model to Pro without further evidence.
Events data gap: Event records only start 2025-03-07. Pre-signal analysis excludes 35/79 tickets (44%) that pre-date this window. Full coverage would require backfilling event history.
May 2025 spike: Cause is unknown. It did not inflate the H4 finding (excluding May strengthens the effect), but it represents an unexplained pattern that should be resolved.

Key queries

Pre-signal H4 (core query):

WITH first_tickets AS (
  SELECT org_id, MIN(opened_at) AS first_ticket_at
  FROM support_tickets
  WHERE opened_at >= '2025-03-07'
  GROUP BY org_id
),
pre_window AS (
  SELECT
    ft.org_id, ft.first_ticket_at,
    COUNT(CASE WHEN e.occurred_at BETWEEN ft.first_ticket_at - INTERVAL '30 days'
                                      AND ft.first_ticket_at THEN 1 END) AS events_30d_before,
    COUNT(CASE WHEN e.occurred_at BETWEEN ft.first_ticket_at - INTERVAL '60 days'
                                      AND ft.first_ticket_at - INTERVAL '30 days' THEN 1 END) AS events_30d_baseline
  FROM first_tickets ft
  LEFT JOIN events e ON e.org_id = ft.org_id
  GROUP BY ft.org_id, ft.first_ticket_at
)
SELECT
  ROUND(AVG(events_30d_before), 1)    AS avg_pre_ticket_events,   -- 11.2
  ROUND(AVG(events_30d_baseline), 1)  AS avg_baseline_events,     -- 8.0
  COUNT(CASE WHEN events_30d_before > events_30d_baseline * 1.5 THEN 1 END) AS spike_accounts  -- 13/29
FROM pre_window;

Category by plan:

SELECT a.plan, st.category, COUNT(*) AS tickets
FROM support_tickets st
JOIN accounts a ON st.org_id = a.id
GROUP BY a.plan, st.category
ORDER BY a.plan, tickets DESC;
Methodology
  • Analysis unit: account (org_id), not individual user. Support tickets link to accounts via org_id.
  • Complaint definition: any account with ≥1 support ticket in the analysis window.
  • Pre-signal: within-account comparison of 30-day pre-ticket event count vs prior 30-day baseline, for each account's first-ever ticket.
