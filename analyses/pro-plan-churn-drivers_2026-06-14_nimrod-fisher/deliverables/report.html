Pro-plan "churn" in 2025 is downsizing, not loss

Zero of 15 Pro accounts fully left Pulseboard in 2025. The cancellations that did happen are concentrated in the $199 subscription tier within its first 90 days — while the $29 entry tier has never churned. Four of five cancelling accounts kept an active subscription. The drivers describe which subscriptions get trimmed, not which customers leave.

Question  What drives Pro-plan churn in 2025? Population  15 Pro accounts · 34 subscriptions Confidence  Medium (small sample)
Why this matters

The decision this supports

Don't fight a churn fire that isn't burning

If leadership reads "30% Pro churn" and launches a broad retention program, they'd be aiming at the wrong target. True customer loss (logo churn) among Pro accounts in 2025 is zero. The real, narrow opportunity is value-realization for new $199 subscriptions in their first quarter — a packaging and onboarding problem, not an engagement or support crisis.

Finding 1 · Hypothesis H1

The $29 tier never churns; everything above runs ~30%

Evidence: Moderate

The entry tier ($29/mo) has never lost a subscription (0/9), while both paid-up tiers churn around 30%. The robust statement is a cliff — $29 vs everything above — not a smooth gradient ($79 and $199 are statistically indistinguishable). Direction is strong and the gap is economically large (risk difference 30.4pp), but with 7 cancellations it is not statistically significant (Fisher's exact p≈0.15). We ruled out a product confound (the same product is sold at all three prices) and a newer-cohort confound (the tiers are the same age).

Finding 2 · Hypothesis H2

$199 subscriptions that fail, fail fast

Evidence: Moderate

Among canceled $199 subscriptions the median time-to-cancel is just 24 days, and 4 of 5 canceled within 90 days (first-90-day hazard 30.8%). The $79 tier shows the opposite pattern — its cancellations come late (median 244 days). So early-life churn is specific to the top price point: it is a first-quarter value-realization problem, not a Pro-wide one.

Finding 3 · Hypothesis H0 — the headline

Cancelling ≠ leaving

Evidence: Strong

Of the five Pro accounts that canceled a subscription, four kept an active subscription and only one fully left — and that exit was in 2024. A SaaS account dropped $79+$199 but kept $79+$199+$199; a FinTech account dropped $79+$199 but kept $199. This is customers trimming their subscription mix, not abandoning Pulseboard.

Finding 4 · Hypotheses H3 & H4

What does not predict churn

H3 · Refuted

Engagement

Cancelling accounts were more active, not less — 47 vs 39 events in the coverage window. The "disengagement precedes churn" idea is the opposite of what the data shows. Do not build an engagement-decline alert. (Coverage-limited: 4 of 7 cancels in window.)

H4 · Null

Support burden

Ticket volume barely differs — 2.0 vs 1.5 tickets per account on tiny counts. Support load does not distinguish churners, consistent with prior Pulseboard analyses. No support-volume churn rule is warranted.

Now what

Recommendations

  1. Target the $199 first-90-day window. Stand up a value-realization play for new $199 subscriptions — a CS check-in by day 30 and day 60 focused on activation, since that is where ~31% are lost.
  2. Reframe internal "Pro churn" reporting. Separate subscription downsizing from logo churn. Logo churn in 2025 is zero; reporting a single ~30% figure mis-signals a crisis.
  3. Review the $199 packaging, not the product. The same product sells fine at $29/$79; the failure is concentrated at the top price's perceived first-quarter value. Treat as pricing/packaging, not feature gaps.
  4. Do not invest in engagement- or support-based churn alerts for Pro — neither carries signal here (engagement runs the wrong way).
  5. Revisit when more cancellations accrue. At n=7, nothing is statistically conclusive. Re-run once the Pro cancel count roughly doubles, or instrument onboarding to test the value-realization hypothesis directly.
Honest limits

Caveats

  • Small sample. 7 cancellations (5 in 2025). All findings are descriptive over the full population; none reach statistical significance.
  • Definition-sensitive. Churn ranges 7.7%–38.5% by definition — always read the rate with its definition.
  • Durability of "0 exits" unknown. The frozen 2025-06-17 snapshot can't see whether rationalized accounts later churn fully. This is the key open question.
  • Engagement is coverage-limited. Event data spans only 2025-03-07 → 06-06; only 4 of 7 cancels fall inside it.
  • Industry not readable. 1–3 Pro accounts per vertical — no reliable industry signal.
  • Data note. 2 of 15 Pro accounts have no subscription rows at all; the product catalog price never matches the real subscription price (price tier taken from subscriptions.monthly_price).
