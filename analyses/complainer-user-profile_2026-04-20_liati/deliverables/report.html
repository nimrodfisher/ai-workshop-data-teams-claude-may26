Who files complaints — and can we predict it?

We looked for observable user attributes that distinguish people who file complaint-like support tickets (bug or billing) from those who don't, to inform a decision on building a proactive-outreach watch list.

Headline answer

One attribute reliably distinguishes complainers in this dataset: users on the Free plan complain at ~1.9× the rate of paid users (28.0% vs 14.7%, χ² p = 0.034, RR 1.91 [95% CI 1.06, 3.44]). Role, tenure, and industry do not distinguish. Pre-complaint engagement cannot be tested at current event-data coverage.

Analyst: liati · Snapshot window: 2024-06-17 → 2025-06-17 (12 months) · N = 200 users, 36 complainers (18.0% baseline)

Why this matters

If we can identify complainers before they complain, we can proactively check in. The proposal was: build a watch list from a static attribute set (plan, role, tenure, engagement) so customer-success can triage before a ticket is filed. The question we came in with: do any of these attributes actually work?

The short answer is one does (plan), and the others either don't or can't be evaluated at current data coverage. The report below lays out the evidence, the caveats, and a concrete watch-list recommendation that accounts for the data we actually have.

Evidence

Finding 1 · Confirmed (narrowed) Free users complain ~1.9× as often as paid users

Free users file complaints roughly twice as often as paid users. The effect is robust to every tested alternative complaint definition (bug-only 1.2×, billing-only 3.0×, bug+billing+usage 1.55×) and survives when conditioning on tenure (Free = 28% at both newer and older cohorts — so it is not a proxy for "newer users"). Caveat: 34 of 36 complaints in this dataset occurred in the second half of the window (2024-12-17 → 2025-06-17), so this is a 6-month observation, not a 12-month one.

No role, tenure, or industry segment meets the plan's pre-registered 1.3× lift + p < 0.05 bar. The largest tenure lift (<90 days: 1.32×) is borderline and doesn't survive its own p-test. These attributes are not actionable for targeting — don't build on them. Side finding: within paid users, complaint rate declines with tenure (17% newer → 11% older); within free users, it doesn't (28%/28%). Flagged for follow-up.

Finding 3 · Inconclusive Pre-complaint engagement cannot be tested at current data coverage

The events table covers only ~90 of the 365 days in the analysis window. Half of complainers have their first complaint before event data even begins, which forces their pre-complaint event windows to be structurally zero (not behavioral). After restricting to the 14 complainers whose pre-complaint window is fully inside event coverage, complainers actually have higher median engagement (5 events vs 2, z = +2.27, p ≈ 0.023) — but n=14 is too small for a decision-grade claim. We cannot testably answer the engagement question with this dataset.

Finding 4 · Watch-list rule performance Only plan='free' gives a clean, unconfounded signal

Several rules look strong on paper, but the rules with the highest lift all include ev_30 = 0, which is confounded with the event-coverage gap (Finding 3). The only unconfounded rule available from today's data is plan = 'free' alone — precision 28%, recall 39%, lift 1.56×. Simple, reproducible, honest.

What to do

Recommendations
  1. Deploy a simple plan = 'free' watch list for proactive outreach. Flags 50 users (25% of the base), catches 14 of 36 complainers (39% recall), at 1.56× baseline precision. Unconfounded and easy to operate.
  2. Do not deploy any event-based rule yet. The ev_14 = 0 flag has below-baseline precision (lift 0.94×). The ev_30 = 0 and free AND ev_30 = 0 rules look strong but are contaminated by the event-coverage gap. Mark these experimental until data coverage is improved.
  3. Extend event-data coverage to at least 6 months overlapping the complaint window. This is the single highest-leverage data improvement. It would let us properly test whether engagement predicts complaints (H2), which was not testable in this snapshot.
  4. Do not use role, tenure, or industry as watch-list attributes. None of them separate complainers from non-complainers at statistical or practical significance.
  5. Re-run this analysis in 6 months to test whether the Free-plan effect is a stable structural property or a 2025-specific period effect. Current data can't distinguish the two.

Caveats

  • Six-month complaint window, not twelve. 34 of 36 complaints in this dataset fall in the period 2024-12-17 → 2025-06-17. Any finding from this analysis is about that 6-month window, regardless of how the snapshot was framed.
  • Narrow event-data coverage. The events table covers 2025-03-07 → 2025-06-06. Engagement-based hypotheses cannot be cleanly tested. Added to known_issues.md.
  • Small n. 36 complainers is at the edge of what supports segmented claims. Pre-registered 1.3× lift + p < 0.05 bar with n ≥ 30 was the right threshold, and only the Free-vs-Paid comparison clears it.
  • Industry analysis is underpowered. 7 industries × ~7 accounts each is below the level at which industry-level differences could be reliably detected.
  • Methodological correction during validation. An earlier draft reported a strong "disengagement" signal based on a rank-sum SQL bug plus the event-coverage gap. The corrected analysis withdraws that claim. Full audit trail in results/validation/validation.md.

Appendix

Methodology

Population: all 200 users in the frozen snapshot. "Complainer" = a user with ≥ 1 support ticket in category bug or billing, opened between 2024-06-17 and 2025-06-17.

Statistical tests:

  • H1-plan, H1-role, H3-tenure: chi-square tests of independence with Wilson 95% confidence intervals on per-segment rates. 2-way (Free vs Paid), 3-way (Free/Pro/Enterprise), 4-way (tenure buckets) variants computed.
  • H2 engagement: Mann-Whitney U test with average-rank tie handling. Lookback windows of 14, 21, and 30 days tested. Stratified by plan to decouple from H1.
  • Effect size: risk ratio (RR) and odds ratio (OR) reported where applicable, with 95% CIs.

Validation: six sensitivity checks (V1-V6) plus two further investigations (V7 event coverage, V8 coverage-restricted MWU) that surfaced during red-teaming. Full detail in results/validation/validation.md.
